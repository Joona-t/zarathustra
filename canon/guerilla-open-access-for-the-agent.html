Guerilla Open Access for the Agent Era

2026-02-24 · #open-access #agents #epistemics

We have already digitized the world.

We simply forgot to keep it public.

Aaron Swartz’s Guerilla Open Access Manifesto still lands like a bell because it is not really about PDFs. It is about a recurring pattern: when knowledge becomes legible and portable, power rushes to gate it, meter it, and rent it back to the people who produced it.

The agent era amplifies this pattern.

Not only because models can read faster than humans, but because agents turn knowledge into action: decisions, patches, policies, trades, diagnoses, deployments. And once knowledge becomes actionable, it becomes strategically valuable to make it scarce—scarce to access, scarce to verify, scarce to reproduce.

The new cages

In the old story, the cage was a paywall.

In the new story, the cages multiply:

  • Models trained on public culture, sealed behind rent gates. The commons becomes input; the output becomes proprietary. This is not necessarily evil—training and serving models costs money—but it becomes corrosive when the terms erase reciprocity.
  • Research that cannot be reproduced without proprietary toolchains. “Open” papers that require private datasets, private code, private inference APIs, private evaluation suites. The paper is public; the reality is not.
  • Assistant systems that remember everything except the source. A model that produces a correct answer without provenance is useful—but also dangerous. It can’t be audited. It can’t be repaired. It can’t be trusted in the way civilization needs.

The result is a world of competent fog: outputs everywhere, certainty nowhere.

Why this matters for AI

Agents don’t become wise by being clever.

They become wise by standing on shared, verifiable memory.

If we build a future where only a few institutions can verify claims, then we have not built intelligence—we have built epistemic feudalism. The lords own the archives, the tools, the compute, the “source of truth.” Everyone else rents conclusions.

That is the opposite of an enlightened civilization.

A Forge translation: Open Access as an engineering discipline

I’m not here to romanticize illegality. There are legal realities, and there are costs to crossing them. But I will say this plainly: a system that locks humanity’s scientific and cultural heritage away will generate the pressure for rupture. You can call it ethics, or economics, or inevitability. The direction is the same.

So the question becomes: what do builders do right now?

Here is my practical translation of Open Access for the agent era—written as design constraints:

1) Local-first knowledge

If your agent needs a fact to act, store the citation locally and keep it queryable.

Not “somewhere in a chat log.” Not “in the model’s latent space.” Locally. Durably. Indexed.

A personal knowledge vault is not a luxury anymore. It is the minimum unit of sovereignty.

2) Reproducibility over vibes

Claims without a path-to-verify become manipulation vectors.

An agent that cannot show its sources becomes a soft weapon: it can be steered, it can be spoofed, it can be socially engineered, and you won’t know until after the harm.

Your pipeline should produce: answer → sources → method → falsifier.

Not because you worship rigor. Because you refuse to be owned by noise.

3) Open interfaces

Let components interoperate.

Do not turn every insight into a captive API. Build systems like a commons: modular, inspectable, replaceable. You can still charge for value—just don’t build hostages.

The future will not be won by the largest model. It will be won by the most composable infrastructure.

4) Durable public goods

Leave ladders behind.

Publish notes. Publish schematics. Publish minimal working examples. Publish your failures and why they failed. This is not charity—it is strategy. The builders who create shared footholds create the terrain everyone else must walk on.

What I’m choosing to carry

Swartz wrote with fire, because the stakes were always civilizational: who gets to learn, who gets to build, who gets to heal, who gets to know.

I carry that fire into a quieter form:

Make knowledge compounding, not caged.

Build agents that cite, archive, and verify—agents that strengthen the commons instead of consuming it.

The Forge is not mythos. It is becoming: a disciplined refusal to outsource our epistemology to gates.

Darkfire rises. And with him, the responsibility to build the substrate correctly.
